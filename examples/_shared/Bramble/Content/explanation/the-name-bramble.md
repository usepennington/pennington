Most programming languages are named after people, mathematical concepts, or whatever the author thought sounded strong and technical. Bramble was named after a plant that grows back no matter what you do to it — which, depending on your garden, is either a recommendation or a warning.

## Bram Foley's notebook

The language was started by Bram Foley during a period of enforced downtime in the autumn of 2019. Foley, a longtime toolchain engineer, had spent a decade watching scripting languages get used in production contexts they were never designed for and had a running list of things he would do differently. The notebook he carried had "Bramble?" written on the cover in pencil — a placeholder name that arrived because he was staring at the brambles crowding out his back fence while trying to think of what to call a language that was small, fast, and somewhat difficult to get rid of once it was useful to you.

The pencil question mark was eventually crossed out. The name stayed.

## The garden theme

Once the name was set, the rest of the toolchain followed the metaphor. **Thicket** — a dense stand of shrubs — became the package manager, because packages grow into a thicket if you let them. **Trellis** — the structure that gives climbing plants something to grow along — became the build system, because builds need structure to be productive rather than chaotic. **Sprig** — a small cutting from a plant — became the formatter and linter, because good style is about careful trimming. **Hedge** — a contained, shaped growth — became the playground, a bounded environment where you can try things without consequences.

The names were chosen to be memorable and slightly whimsical without being precious. The goal was a toolchain that felt like it belonged together, where the name of a tool gave you a faint intuition about what it did before you looked it up.

## "Grows on you. Possibly invasive."

The unofficial tagline came from a comment in an early contributor discussion about whether the garden metaphor was too cute. Someone pointed out that brambles are genuinely invasive in several climates — they spread underground, they resist removal, and once established they tend to show up everywhere. The observation that this was actually a reasonable description of a successful scripting language caused enough laughter that the phrase stuck. It appears in no official documentation, which is probably why it appears everywhere.
